Pronomi — Italian Pronouns

Complete guide to subject, direct object, indirect object, combined, reflexive, and relative pronouns — with placement rules, agreement, and real-world examples
Subject Pronouns (Pronomi Soggetto)
Italian subject pronouns identify who performs the action. Unlike English, they are usually omitted because verb endings already encode the subject. They appear mainly for emphasis, contrast, or clarity.
Forms
Six persons — singular and plural. Note that lui/lei replaced the older egli/ella in everyday speech, and Lei (capitalized) is the formal "you."
Person Pronoun English Notes
1st sing. io I Never capitalized mid-sentence (unlike English "I")
2nd sing. tu you (informal) Used with friends, family, peers, children
3rd sing. lui / lei / Lei he / she / you (formal) Lei (formal) takes 3rd-person verb forms
1st pl. noi we Sometimes replaced by impersonal "si" in speech
2nd pl. voi you (plural) Also used as formal plural in some regions
3rd pl. loro they Loro (capitalized) is ultra-formal plural "you" (rare)
When to Omit vs. Include the Subject Pronoun
Omit (default): The verb ending already tells the listener who the subject is — "parlo" can only mean "I speak."
Include for emphasis: "Io parlo italiano" stresses that I speak Italian (maybe you don't).
Include for contrast: "Io lavoro, tu dormi" — I work while you sleep.
Include for clarity: In 3rd person, "lui parla" vs. "lei parla" removes ambiguity when both people were mentioned.
Tu vs. Lei — The Formal/Informal Distinction
Tu is used with anyone you would call by first name: friends, family, children, colleagues of similar age.
Lei is used with strangers, elders, professionals, officials, and anyone you address with a title (Signore, Dottore, Professore). It takes 3rd-person singular verb forms — as if you were talking about "she/he." In writing, Lei is capitalized to distinguish it from "lei" (she).
Examples
omitted Parlo italiano abbastanza bene dopo tre anni di studio.
I speak Italian fairly well after three years of study. (no pronoun needed — verb ending is clear)
io Io non ho detto niente, è stata Maria a parlare con il professore.
I didn't say anything — it was Maria who spoke with the professor. (emphasis: it wasn't me)
tu vs. io Tu vai al supermercato e io preparo la cena per gli ospiti.
You go to the supermarket and I'll prepare dinner for the guests. (contrast between two people)
Lei Lei desidera un caffè o preferisce un tè, Signora Bianchi?
Would you like a coffee or do you prefer tea, Mrs. Bianchi? (formal — 3rd person verb)
lui/lei Lui dice che ha ragione, ma lei non è d'accordo con la sua opinione.
He says he's right, but she doesn't agree with his opinion. (clarity — distinguishing two people)
Tip: If you are unsure whether to use tu or Lei with someone, start with Lei. The other person will invite you to switch: "Diamoci del tu!" (Let's use tu with each other!). In modern Italy, younger people and informal workplaces default to tu much more quickly.
Direct Object Pronouns (Pronomi Oggetto Diretto)
Direct object pronouns replace the noun that directly receives the action of the verb (no preposition between verb and noun). English equivalents: me, you, him, her, it, us, them.
Forms
lo and la elide to l' before verbs starting with a vowel or "h." The plural forms li and le do not elide.
Person Pronoun English Example Verb Phrase
1st sing. mi me mi vede (sees me)
2nd sing. ti you (informal) ti chiamo (I call you)
3rd sing. m. lo him / it (m.) lo compro (I buy it)
3rd sing. f. la her / it (f.) la leggo (I read it)
3rd sing. formal La you (formal) La ringrazio (I thank you)
1st pl. ci us ci invita (invites us)
2nd pl. vi you (plural) vi aspetto (I wait for you)
3rd pl. m. li them (m.) li conosco (I know them)
3rd pl. f. le them (f.) le vedo (I see them)
Placement Rules
Before a conjugated verb: Lo mangio. (I eat it.)
Attached to an infinitive (drop the final -e): Voglio mangiarlo. (I want to eat it.)
Attached to a gerund: Sto mangiandolo. (I'm eating it.)
Attached to informal imperative (tu/noi/voi): Mangialo! (Eat it!)
Before formal imperative (Lei): Lo mangi! (Eat it! — formal)
With modal verbs, either position is correct: Lo voglio comprare / Voglio comprarlo.
Agreement with Passato Prossimo
When lo, la, li, le precede a verb in the passato prossimo (with avere), the past participle must agree in gender and number with the pronoun.
mi, ti, ci, vi may optionally agree, but agreement is not required.
Examples
lo Conosci quel ristorante vicino alla stazione? Sì, lo conosco molto bene.
Do you know that restaurant near the station? Yes, I know it very well.
la Hai comprato la torta per la festa? Sì, l'ho comprata stamattina al forno.
Did you buy the cake for the party? Yes, I bought it this morning at the bakery. (comprata agrees with la)
li Dove sono i biglietti per il concerto? Li ho messi nel cassetto della scrivania.
Where are the concert tickets? I put them in the desk drawer. (messi agrees with li, masc. pl.)
le Hai visto le chiavi di casa? No, non le ho viste da ieri sera.
Have you seen the house keys? No, I haven't seen them since last night. (viste agrees with le, fem. pl.)
attached Questa pizza è deliziosa — devi assolutamente assaggiarla prima che si freddi.
This pizza is delicious — you absolutely must try it before it gets cold. (la attached to infinitive)
mi Marco mi ha chiamato tre volte ieri sera ma non ho potuto rispondere.
Marco called me three times last night but I couldn't answer.
Common mistake: Forgetting participle agreement. "Ho comprato la torta" becomes "L'ho comprata" (not "L'ho comprato"). The participle must match the gender/number of lo/la/li/le.
Indirect Object Pronouns (Pronomi Oggetto Indiretto)
Indirect object pronouns replace the person to whom or for whom the action is done. In Italian, the indirect object is the noun preceded by the preposition "a" (to). English equivalents: to me, to you, to him/her, to us, to them.
Forms
Notice that mi, ti, ci, vi are identical to the direct object forms. The difference is only in 3rd person: gli (to him), le (to her), Le (to you formal), and gli (to them — replaces the older "loro").
Person Pronoun English Replaces
1st sing. mi to me a me
2nd sing. ti to you (informal) a te
3rd sing. m. gli to him a lui
3rd sing. f. le to her a lei
3rd sing. formal Le to you (formal) a Lei
1st pl. ci to us a noi
2nd pl. vi to you (plural) a voi
3rd pl. gli (loro) to them a loro
Common Verbs That Take Indirect Objects
Many Italian verbs take an indirect object where English uses a direct object. The key pattern: if the Italian verb uses "a" + person, you need an indirect pronoun.

dire a (to say/tell to) — Gli dico la verità.
dare a (to give to) — Le do il libro.
chiedere a (to ask to) — Gli chiedo un favore.
telefonare a (to phone to) — Le telefono stasera.
scrivere a (to write to) — Gli scrivo una lettera.
rispondere a (to answer to) — Gli rispondo domani.
piacere a (to be pleasing to) — Mi piace la musica.
sembrare a (to seem to) — Mi sembra strano.
mancare a (to be missing to) — Mi manca l'Italia.
Direct vs. Indirect — How to Tell the Difference
Direct object: Answers "whom?" or "what?" — Vedo Marco. → Lo vedo.
Indirect object: Answers "to whom?" or "for whom?" — Parlo a Marco. → Gli parlo.

Test: If you can insert "to" or "for" before the person in English, it is probably indirect in Italian. "I tell him" = "I tell to him" → indirect → gli dico.
Examples
gli Ho incontrato Luca al bar e gli ho raccontato tutto quello che è successo ieri.
I met Luca at the bar and told him everything that happened yesterday.
le Quando vedi la professoressa, le puoi chiedere se l'esame è stato spostato?
When you see the professor, can you ask her if the exam has been postponed?
mi La musica classica mi piace molto, soprattutto le sinfonie di Beethoven.
I like classical music a lot, especially Beethoven's symphonies. (lit: classical music is pleasing to me)
ci I nostri genitori ci hanno dato dei buoni consigli prima di partire per l'università.
Our parents gave us good advice before we left for university.
Le Le posso offrire qualcosa da bere, Signor Rossi, un caffè o un bicchiere d'acqua?
Can I offer you something to drink, Mr. Rossi, a coffee or a glass of water? (formal)
Watch out with "piacere": In "Mi piace il gelato," mi is an indirect object (to me), and il gelato is the subject. The verb agrees with the thing liked, not the person: "Mi piace il gelato" but "Mi piacciono i dolci" (plural subject → piacciono).
Combined Pronouns (Pronomi Combinati)
When a verb has both an indirect and a direct object pronoun, they combine into a single unit. The indirect always comes first. Some forms change spelling when combined.
Combination Rules
mi → me before lo/la/li/le/ne
ti → te before lo/la/li/le/ne
gli / le / Le → glie- (fused into one word: glielo, gliela, glieli, gliele, gliene)
ci → ce before lo/la/li/le/ne
vi → ve before lo/la/li/le/ne
Full Combination Table
Read across: indirect pronoun (row) + direct pronoun (column) = combined form.
Indirect ↓ / Direct → lo la li le ne
mi (to me) me lo me la me li me le me ne
ti (to you) te lo te la te li te le te ne
gli / le / Le (to him/her/you formal) glielo gliela glieli gliele gliene
ci (to us) ce lo ce la ce li ce le ce ne
vi (to you pl.) ve lo ve la ve li ve le ve ne
gli (to them) glielo gliela glieli gliele gliene
Placement with Compound Tenses
In compound tenses (passato prossimo, etc.), combined pronouns go before the auxiliary (avere/essere), and past participle agreement follows the same rules as with single direct object pronouns.
Example: "La torta? Gliel'ho data ieri." — The cake? I gave it to him/her yesterday. (data agrees with la)
Examples
glielo Marco voleva il mio numero di telefono e io gliel'ho dato senza pensarci troppo.
Marco wanted my phone number and I gave it to him without thinking too much about it. (gli + lo = glielo)
me la Questa canzone è bellissima — chi me l'ha consigliata? Ah sì, è stata Giulia!
This song is beautiful — who recommended it to me? Oh right, it was Giulia! (mi + la = me la)
te li I documenti per il visto? Te li mando per email appena torno in ufficio domani.
The visa documents? I'll send them to you by email as soon as I get back to the office tomorrow. (ti + li = te li)
ce lo Il professore ce lo ha spiegato tre volte ma nessuno in classe ha capito l'argomento.
The professor explained it to us three times but nobody in class understood the topic. (ci + lo = ce lo)
gliene Se Marco vuole delle mele, gliene porto un paio dal mercato questo pomeriggio.
If Marco wants some apples, I'll bring him a couple from the market this afternoon. (gli + ne = gliene)
Key point: "Gli" (to him), "le" (to her), and "gli" (to them) all merge the same way: glielo, gliela, glieli, gliele, gliene. Context tells you whether the meaning is "to him," "to her," or "to them." In writing, you may see "Glielo" capitalized for the formal "Le" form.
Reflexive Pronouns (Pronomi Riflessivi)
Reflexive pronouns indicate that the subject and object of the verb are the same person — the action "reflects" back onto the doer. Italian uses reflexive verbs far more frequently than English.
Forms
Reflexive pronouns look identical to direct/indirect object pronouns in the 1st and 2nd person. Only the 3rd person has a unique form: si.
Person Reflexive Pronoun English Example with "lavarsi"
1st sing. mi myself mi lavo (I wash myself)
2nd sing. ti yourself ti lavi (you wash yourself)
3rd sing. si himself / herself / itself / yourself (formal) si lava (he/she washes himself/herself)
1st pl. ci ourselves ci laviamo (we wash ourselves)
2nd pl. vi yourselves vi lavate (you wash yourselves)
3rd pl. si themselves si lavano (they wash themselves)
True Reflexive vs. Reciprocal
True reflexive: The subject acts on itself — "Mi vesto" (I dress myself).
Reciprocal: Two or more people act on each other — "Ci vediamo domani" (We'll see each other tomorrow). Reciprocal uses the same pronouns (ci, vi, si) but requires a plural subject.

To clarify reciprocal meaning, add "l'un l'altro" (each other, m.) or "l'una l'altra" (each other, f.): "Si aiutano l'un l'altro" (They help each other).
Common Reflexive Verbs
alzarsi — to get up
svegliarsi — to wake up
lavarsi — to wash oneself
vestirsi — to get dressed
sentirsi — to feel
trovarsi — to find oneself / to be located
chiamarsi — to be called (name)
divertirsi — to enjoy oneself / have fun
arrabbiarsi — to get angry
preoccuparsi — to worry
Compound Tenses: Reflexives Use "essere"
All reflexive verbs form compound tenses with essere (not avere), and the past participle agrees with the subject in gender and number.
Example: "Maria si è svegliata presto" (Maria woke up early — svegliata, fem. sing.)
Examples
mi Ogni mattina mi sveglio alle sei e mezza e poi faccio colazione con calma.
Every morning I wake up at six thirty and then have breakfast calmly.
si Mia sorella si è laureata in medicina l'anno scorso con il massimo dei voti.
My sister graduated in medicine last year with the highest marks. (laureata agrees with sorella)
ci (reciprocal) Io e Francesca ci scriviamo messaggi ogni giorno anche se viviamo in città diverse.
Francesca and I write messages to each other every day even though we live in different cities.
si I bambini si sono divertiti tantissimo alla festa di compleanno di sabato scorso.
The children had a great time at last Saturday's birthday party. (divertiti agrees with bambini, masc. pl.)
ti Non ti preoccupare troppo per l'esame — hai studiato abbastanza e andrà tutto bene.
Don't worry too much about the exam — you studied enough and everything will be fine.
Important: Many Italian verbs are reflexive where English is not: "chiamarsi" (to be called, lit. to call oneself), "annoiarsi" (to get bored), "accorgersi" (to notice). Always check whether an Italian verb is reflexive — using the wrong auxiliary (avere instead of essere) is a common error.
Relative Pronouns (Pronomi Relativi)
Relative pronouns connect a main clause to a relative clause, referring back to a noun in the main clause. They are the Italian equivalents of "who," "which," "that," and "whom."
Forms and Usage
Pronoun English Used For Example
che who / which / that Subject or direct object of the relative clause (people and things) Il libro che leggo... (The book that I read...)
cui whom / which (after preposition) After a preposition: a cui, di cui, con cui, in cui, per cui, etc. La persona a cui parlo... (The person to whom I speak...)
il quale / la quale who / which (formal) More formal/written alternative to che/cui; agrees in gender/number Il ragazzo il quale studia... (The boy who studies...)
i quali / le quali who / which (pl. formal) Plural of il quale/la quale Le ragazze le quali studiano... (The girls who study...)
chi whoever / those who / he/she who Combines antecedent + relative pronoun; always singular verb Chi dorme non piglia pesci. (He who sleeps catches no fish.)
When to Use Each Pronoun
"che" is the most common — use it for both subject and direct object when there is no preposition. It is invariable (no gender/number change).

"cui" is required after prepositions. Without a preposition, "cui" = "a cui" (the "a" is implied). It is also invariable.
Possessive "cui": article + cui + noun = "whose" — "Il ragazzo il cui padre è medico" (The boy whose father is a doctor).

"il quale / la quale / i quali / le quali" replaces both "che" and "cui" in formal or ambiguous contexts. It must agree with the noun it refers to, which helps clarify meaning in complex sentences.

"chi" is used without an antecedent — it stands alone meaning "the person who" or "whoever." Always takes a singular verb.
Examples
che (subject) La ragazza che abita nell'appartamento di fronte al mio è una pianista professionista.
The girl who lives in the apartment across from mine is a professional pianist.
che (object) Il film che abbiamo visto al cinema sabato sera mi ha fatto riflettere molto.
The film that we saw at the cinema on Saturday night made me think a lot.
in cui Il ristorante in cui abbiamo cenato la settimana scorsa ha chiuso per ristrutturazione.
The restaurant in which we had dinner last week has closed for renovation.
di cui La questione di cui stiamo parlando richiede molta attenzione da parte di tutti.
The matter about which we are speaking requires a lot of attention from everyone.
il cui Ho un amico il cui fratello lavora come ingegnere alla Ferrari a Maranello.
I have a friend whose brother works as an engineer at Ferrari in Maranello.
chi Chi trova un amico trova un tesoro — è uno dei proverbi italiani più conosciuti.
Whoever finds a friend finds a treasure — it's one of the best-known Italian proverbs.
Common error: Using "che" after a preposition. You cannot say "la persona con che parlo" — it must be "la persona con cui parlo" or "la persona con la quale parlo." The pronoun "che" never follows a preposition.
Pronoun Placement Rules (Posizione dei Pronomi)
Italian pronoun placement depends on the verb form. The rules apply equally to direct, indirect, reflexive, and combined pronouns. Mastering placement is essential for natural-sounding Italian.
Rule 1: Before Conjugated Verbs
Pronouns go immediately before any conjugated (finite) verb form — present, past, future, subjunctive, conditional, etc. Nothing can come between the pronoun and the verb except another pronoun.
Lo mangio. (I eat it.) — Non lo mangio. (I don't eat it.) — "non" comes before the pronoun.
Rule 2: Attached to Infinitives, Gerunds, and Ecco
Pronouns attach to the end of infinitives (the infinitive drops its final -e), gerunds, and the word "ecco."
Infinitive: mangiare + lo → mangiarlo
Gerund: mangiando + lo → mangiandolo
Ecco: ecco + lo → eccolo (here it is!)

With modal verbs (potere, volere, dovere, sapere), you have a choice — pronoun before the modal or attached to the infinitive:
Lo posso fare = Posso farlo (both correct, same meaning)
Rule 3: Imperative — Informal vs. Formal
Informal imperative (tu, noi, voi): Pronouns attach to the end of the verb.
Mangialo! (Eat it!) — Diciamoglielo! (Let's tell it to him!) — Scrivile! (Write to her!)

Formal imperative (Lei, Loro): Pronouns go before the verb.
Lo mangi! (Eat it! — formal) — Glielo dica! (Tell it to him! — formal)

Negative tu imperative: Both positions are possible.
Non mangiarlo! = Non lo mangiare! (Don't eat it! — both correct)
Rule 4: Double Pronoun Order
When two pronouns appear together, they always follow this order:
Indirect object → Direct object → Verb
or Reflexive → Direct object → Verb

Me lo dai? (Will you give it to me?) — indirect (me) + direct (lo)
Se lo compra. (He buys it for himself.) — reflexive (se) + direct (lo)
Examples — Placement in Context
before conjugated Gli telefono stasera dopo cena per chiedergli se viene alla festa di sabato.
I'll call him tonight after dinner to ask him if he's coming to Saturday's party.
attached to infinitive Devo comprarle un regalo speciale perché domani è il suo compleanno.
I have to buy her a special gift because tomorrow is her birthday. (le attached to comprare)
attached to gerund Stavo parlandone proprio adesso con il mio collega quando sei arrivato tu.
I was just talking about it with my colleague when you arrived. (ne attached to parlando)
informal imperative Questa lettera è importante — spediscigliela il prima possibile, per favore!
This letter is important — send it to him as soon as possible, please! (gliela attached to spedisci)
formal imperative Signora, si accomodi pure e mi dica come posso aiutarLa oggi.
Ma'am, please sit down and tell me how I can help you today. (si and mi before formal imperative)
Tip for monosyllabic tu-imperative forms: With da', di', fa', sta', va' (informal imperatives of dare, dire, fare, stare, andare), the first consonant of the attached pronoun doubles: da' + mi → dammi, di' + lo → dillo, fa' + ci → facci. Exception: "gli" never doubles — dagli (not daggli).
Common Mistakes with Pronouns
These are the errors that Italian learners make most frequently. Study them to avoid falling into the same traps.
1. Confusing "gli" (to him/them) with "le" (to her)
In spoken Italian, gli is increasingly used for both "to him" and "to them" — and many speakers also use it for "to her" (instead of le). In careful speech and writing, maintain the distinction:
Gli parlo = I speak to him / to them
Le parlo = I speak to her
Mixing them up is very common even among native speakers, but using "le" correctly for "to her" marks you as precise.
2. Confusing "lo" (direct object) with "gli" (indirect object)
Lo vedo = I see him (direct object — no preposition)
Gli parlo = I speak to him (indirect object — preposition "a")

Ask yourself: does the Italian verb take a direct object or use "a" + person? "Vedere" takes a direct object; "parlare" takes "a" + person.
3. Forgetting Participle Agreement with lo/la/li/le
When lo, la, li, or le precede the passato prossimo, the past participle must agree:
Correct: Le mele? Le ho comprate. (The apples? I bought them.)
Wrong: Le mele? Le ho comprato.
This is mandatory, not optional. With mi, ti, ci, vi, agreement is optional.
4. Using the Wrong Pronoun with "piacere"
"Piacere" works backwards compared to English "to like." The person who likes is the indirect object:
Correct: Mi piace il calcio. (I like soccer. — lit: soccer is pleasing to me)
Wrong: Io piaccio il calcio. (This means "I am pleasing to soccer" — nonsense!)

The same applies to sembrare, mancare, servire, bastare, interessare, and other "backwards" verbs.
5. Using "che" After a Preposition
Wrong: La persona con che parlo...
Correct: La persona con cui parlo... / La persona con la quale parlo...

"Che" can never follow a preposition. Always use "cui" or "il quale/la quale" after prepositions.
6. Forgetting "essere" with Reflexive Compound Tenses
All reflexive verbs use essere (not avere) in compound tenses, and the participle agrees with the subject:
Correct: Maria si è svegliata presto. (Maria woke up early.)
Wrong: Maria si ha svegliato presto.
This is one of the most frequent errors for English speakers learning Italian.
Remember: Pronouns are the glue of fluent Italian. Start by mastering subject and direct object pronouns, then add indirect and reflexive. Combined pronouns and relative pronouns will follow naturally with practice. When in doubt, identify whether the verb takes a direct object (no preposition) or an indirect object (with "a") — this determines which pronoun to use.
