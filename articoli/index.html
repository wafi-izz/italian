🇮🇹 Italian Articles

Complete guide to definite, indefinite, and partitive articles with rules, forms, and usage examples
Definite Articles (il, lo, la, i, gli, le)
Used for specific, known nouns. Equivalent to English "the". Must agree in gender and number.
Forms and Rules
Complex sounds (s+consonant, z, gn, ps, x, pn, i+vowel, y) require special masculine forms. Vowels cause contraction (l').

Feminine Singular la — l' la casa, l'amica
Masculine Plural i gli
(s+cons, z, gn, ps, x, pn, i+vowel, y)		 — i libri, gli zaini
Feminine Plural le — — le case, le amiche
Complex Sounds Reference
Words starting with: gn, i+vowel, pn, ps, s+consonant, x, y, z
gn: lo gnocco (gnocchi), gli gnocchi
The dumpling, the dumplings
s+cons: lo stadio, gli stadi
The stadium, the stadiums
z: lo zoo, gli zoo
The zoo, the zoos
Usage Cases
General sense: L'acqua è essenziale. (Water is essential)
Specific items: Ho comprato il libro. (I bought the book)
Geography: Vivo in Italia. (I live in Italy)
Titles: Conosco il dottore Rossi. (I know Doctor Rossi)
Professions: Faccio lo scrittore. (I'm a writer)
Time: Cucino il venerdì. (I cook on Fridays)
Parts of body: Mi lavo i capelli. (I wash my hair)
Indefinite Articles (un, uno, una, dei, degli, delle)
Used for unspecified, unknown nouns. Equivalent to English "a/an" or plural "some".
Forms and Rules
Same rules as definite articles for complex sounds and vowels. Plural forms mean "some".
Note: Plural forms are contractions of "di" + definite article.

(s+cons, z, gn, ps, x, pn, i+vowel, y)		 un — un libro, uno zaino, un amico
Feminine Singular una — un' — una casa, un'amica
Masculine Plural (some) dei degli — di + i
di + gli		 dei libri, degli zaini
Feminine Plural (some) delle — — di + le delle case, delle amiche
Usage Cases
Unspecified items: Ho comprato un libro. (I bought a book)
Professions: Sono un insegnante. (I'm a teacher) — optional in some regions
With adjectives: È un libro interessante. (It's an interesting book)
Plural "some": Ci sono delle mele. (There are some apples)
Special rule: With professions after essere (to be), the article is often omitted in spoken Italian, especially in southern regions: Sono insegnante vs Sono un insegnante.
Partitive Articles (del, dello, della, dei, degli, delle)
Used for unspecified quantities of uncountable nouns. Equivalent to English "some/any" with mass nouns.
Forms and Rules
Formed by combining preposition "di" with definite articles. Used with food, liquids, and abstract quantities.
Note: These are contractions where "di" + definite article combine into one word.

Feminine Singular della — dell' di + la
di + l'		 della pasta, dell'acqua

(s+cons, z, gn, ps, x, pn, i+vowel, y)		 — di + i
di + gli		 dei pomodori, degli spinaci
Feminine Plural delle — — di + le delle mele, delle carote
Usage Cases
Food & drink: Vorrei del pane. (I'd like some bread)
Liquids: Bevo dell'acqua. (I drink some water)
Abstract quantities: Ha del talento. (He has some talent)
Negative: Non voglio pane. (I don't want any bread) — partitive dropped in negative sentences
Key difference: Partitive articles are for uncountable quantities (del pane = some bread), while indefinite plural is for countable items (dei pani = some breads/loaves).
Articulated Prepositions
Prepositions that contract with definite articles. These are required in Italian (unlike English contractions which are optional).
What are Articulated Prepositions?
Grammatical type: Preposition + definite article contractions
Purpose: Combine preposition with definite article into one word
Usage: Required in Italian (not optional like English "don't")
Main prepositions: a, da, di, in, su, con

con (with) + Article
⚠️ Unlike the other prepositions, "con" almost never contracts in modern Italian. The separated forms (con il, con lo, con la...) are standard. Only col (con + il) survives in informal speech. Avoid "collo" (= neck!), "colla" (= glue!), and "colle" (= hill!) — they are archaic and will confuse people.

Common Examples
a: Vado al cinema (I go to the cinema), Abito alla periferia (I live in the suburbs), Vado allo stadio (I go to the stadium)
da: Vengo dal mercato (I come from the market), Lavoro da casa (I work from home), Vengo dallo zio (I come from my uncle)
di: La casa della nonna (Grandma's house), Un amico degli amici (A friend of friends), Il libro dello studente (The student's book)
in: Vivo nel centro (I live in the center), Andiamo nelle montagne (We go to the mountains), Abito nello stesso palazzo (I live in the same building)
su: Il libro sul tavolo (The book on the table), Cammino sui marciapiedi (I walk on sidewalks), Siediti sullo sgabello (Sit on the stool)
con: Vado con il treno (I go by train), Parlo con i colleghi (I speak with colleagues), Esco con lo zio (I go out with my uncle)
Important: Unlike English contractions (I'm, don't), Italian articulated prepositions are mandatory and cannot be separated. You must use "al cinema" not "a il cinema".
How "About" Translates in Italian
The English word "about" has multiple translations in Italian depending on context.
1. "About" = Regarding/Concerning (di, su, riguardo a)
Used when talking about topics, subjects, or what something is concerning.
Parliamo di politica. (We talk about politics)
About = di (of/about)
Common with parlare di, trattare di
Un libro sull'Italia. (A book about Italy)
About = su + l' (on/about) — contracted form is mandatory
For books, movies, discussions about topics
Riguardo a questo problema... (Regarding this problem...)
About = riguardo a (regarding/concerning)
More formal, often in writing
2. "About" = Approximately (circa, più o meno, intorno a)
Used for approximate numbers, quantities, or time.
Costa circa 100 euro. (It costs about 100 euros)
About = circa (approximately)
Most common for numbers and prices
Arrivo verso le 5. (I'll arrive about 5 o'clock)
About = verso (around/toward)
For approximate time
Più o meno 50 persone. (About 50 people)
About = più o meno (more or less)
For rough estimates
3. "About" = Around/Surrounding (intorno a, attorno a)
Used for physical location or surrounding area.
Camminiamo intorno al parco. (We walk about the park)
About = intorno a (around)
Physical movement around an area
Gironzolava attorno alla casa. (He was wandering about the house)
About = attorno a (around/about)
Similar to intorno a, slightly more literary
4. "About" = On the point of/Almost (per, sul punto di)
Used in expressions meaning "about to" or "on the verge of".
Stavo per partire. (I was about to leave)
About to = per (on the point of)
Common with stare + per + infinitive
Era sul punto di piangere. (She was about to cry)
About to = sul punto di (on the point of)
More formal/literary than "per"
Key Tip: The translation of "about" depends entirely on context. Always consider what meaning you want to convey - is it "regarding", "approximately", "around", or "about to"?
Preposition Differences: "sul" vs "di"
Understanding when to use "sul" (about/regarding) versus "di" (of) for different meanings.
"sul" = About/Regarding/Concerning
Used when talking about topics, subjects, or what something is concerning. Equivalent to English "about" in the sense of "regarding" or "on the subject of".
Informazioni sul progetto. (Information about the project)
About = sul (regarding/concerning the project)
Focuses on the topic or subject matter
Un libro sul Rinascimento. (A book about the Renaissance)
About = sul (on the subject of the Renaissance)
Common for books, movies, discussions about topics
"di" = Of (Possessive/Descriptive)
Used for possession, origin, or description. Equivalent to English "of" in possessive constructions or when describing what something consists of.
Informazioni del progetto. (Information of the project)
Of = del (belonging to/coming from the project)
Possessive meaning - the information that belongs to the project
Il colore della macchina. (The color of the car)
Of = della (possessive - the car's color)
Describes what belongs to or characterizes something
Key Difference: "sul progetto" means "about the project" (regarding the topic), while "del progetto" means "of the project" (belonging to/coming from the project). The choice changes the entire meaning of the sentence.
Special Rules & Exceptions
Important exceptions and regional variations in article usage.
Other Contractions
Some prepositions form contractions with other words (not just articles).
con + me/te = con me/con te — No change, but pronunciation flows
With me/with you
di + questo = di questo — No contraction, but "d'" before vowels
Of this
Omissions
Before proper names: Vivo a Roma (not "a la Roma")
With professions (regional): Sono medico vs Sono un medico
In lists: Ho comprato pane, latte e uova
With che: Che libro! (not "il che libro")
With possessive adjectives: La mia casa (not "il la mia casa")
Regional Variations
Northern Italy: More likely to use articles with professions
Southern Italy: Often omit articles with professions and in casual speech
Tuscany: May use "i" instead of "gli" in some cases
Written Italian: More formal, tends to include optional articles
