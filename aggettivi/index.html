Aggettivi — Italian Adjectives

A comprehensive guide to Italian adjectives: agreement, position, special forms, comparatives, superlatives, possessives, and demonstratives.
Concordanza — Agreement
Italian adjectives must agree in gender (masculine/feminine) and number (singular/plural) with the noun they modify. There are two main groups of adjectives, each with its own set of endings.
Group 1: Four-Form Adjectives (-o / -a / -i / -e)
These adjectives have four distinct endings — one for each combination of gender and number. The masculine singular ends in -o.
SingularPlural
Masculinealtoalti
Femininealtaalte
Other common Group 1 adjectives: basso, bello, bianco, brutto, caro, freddo, largo, lungo, nero, nuovo, piccolo, rosso, vecchio.
Group 2: Two-Form Adjectives (-e / -i)
These adjectives have only two forms — one for singular (both genders) and one for plural (both genders). The singular ends in -e.
SingularPlural
Masculinegrandegrandi
Femininegrandegrandi
Other common Group 2 adjectives: intelligente, interessante, importante, felice, triste, veloce, difficile, facile, gentile, forte.
Mixed Groups and Gender Precedence
When an adjective modifies nouns of mixed gender, the masculine plural form takes precedence. This applies even when there are more feminine nouns than masculine ones.

Posizione — Position of Adjectives
In Italian the default position for most adjectives is after the noun. However, certain common adjectives typically go before the noun, and some adjectives change meaning depending on their position.
Default Position: After the Noun
Most descriptive adjectives — especially those indicating color, shape, nationality, religion, or technical qualities — follow the noun.

Forme Speciali — Bello, Buono, Grande, Santo
Four common adjectives have special shortened forms when placed before the noun. Their endings follow patterns similar to the definite or indefinite articles.
Bello (Beautiful) — Like the Definite Article
When bello comes before a noun, its forms mirror those of the definite article (il, lo, l', la, i, gli, le).
Before ConsonantBefore S+cons. / ZBefore Vowel

When buono comes before a singular noun, its forms mirror those of the indefinite article (un, uno, una, un'). Plural forms are regular (buoni, buone).

Comparativi — Comparatives
Italian uses three types of comparison: majority (more than), minority (less than), and equality (as...as). A key difficulty is choosing between di and che.
Comparativo di Maggioranza (Majority)
Formed with più + adjective + di / che.
StructureExampleTranslation
più + adj + diRoma è più grande di Milano.Rome is bigger than Milan.
più + adj + di + pronounLui è più alto di me.He is taller than me.
più + adj + che + adjÈ più furbo che intelligente.He is more cunning than intelligent.
Comparativo di Minoranza (Minority)
Formed with meno + adjective + di / che. Works exactly like the majority comparative but expresses "less than."
StructureExampleTranslation
meno + adj + diMilano è meno grande di Roma.Milan is less big than Rome.
meno + adj + cheÈ meno costoso che pericoloso.It is less expensive than dangerous.
Comparativo di Uguaglianza (Equality)
Two equivalent structures: tanto...quanto and così...come. The first word (tanto/così) is often omitted in speech.
StructureExampleTranslation
(tanto) + adj + quantoMarco è (tanto) alto quanto Luca.Marco is as tall as Luca.
(così) + adj + comeMaria è (così) bella come sua madre.Maria is as beautiful as her mother.
Di vs. Che — When to Use Each
This is one of the most common errors learners make. The choice depends on what follows the comparison word.
UseContextExample
DIBefore nounsRoma è più bella di Napoli.
DIBefore pronounsSei più giovane di me.
DIBefore numbersHo più di venti libri.
CHEBefore adjectivesÈ più bello che intelligente.
CHEBefore verbs (infinitives)È più facile parlare che scrivere.
CHEBefore prepositionsCi sono più turisti a Roma che a Milano.
CHEComparing two qualities of same thingQuesta pizza è più buona che bella.
Rule of thumb: use di when comparing two different things/people (two nouns or pronouns). Use che when comparing two qualities, actions, or locations related to the same subject.
La Torre Eiffel è più alta del Colosseo, ma il Colosseo è più antico.
The Eiffel Tower is taller than the Colosseum, but the Colosseum is more ancient.
Secondo me, viaggiare in treno è meno stressante che guidare nelle ore di punta.
In my opinion, traveling by train is less stressful than driving during rush hour.
Mia sorella è tanto intelligente quanto simpatica, e tutti la adorano.
My sister is as intelligent as she is likeable, and everyone adores her.
Questo ristorante è più caro di quello dove siamo andati la settimana scorsa.
This restaurant is more expensive than the one we went to last week.
In estate ci sono più turisti a Firenze che a Bologna, anche se Bologna è bellissima.
In summer there are more tourists in Florence than in Bologna, even though Bologna is very beautiful.
Imparare l'italiano è più divertente che difficile, se si studia con passione ogni giorno.
Learning Italian is more fun than difficult, if you study with passion every day.
Superlativi — Superlatives
Italian has two kinds of superlative: the relative superlative (the most/least ... of a group) and the absolute superlative (extremely, very — no comparison to a group).
Superlativo Relativo (Relative Superlative)
Formed with the definite article + più / meno + adjective + di. Indicates the highest or lowest degree within a specific group.
StructureExampleTranslation
il/la più + adj + diRoma è la più grande città d'Italia.Rome is the largest city in Italy.
il/la meno + adj + diÈ il meno costoso dei tre ristoranti.It is the least expensive of the three restaurants.
noun + più + adjIl giorno più bello della mia vita.The most beautiful day of my life.
The article agrees with the noun, not the adjective: la ragazza più alta, il ragazzo più alto, le ragazze più alte. The preposition di often contracts with the article: di + il = del, di + la = della, etc.
Superlativo Assoluto (Absolute Superlative)
Expresses the highest degree without comparing to a group. Three main ways to form it:

molto + adjmolto + adjectivemolto bello
Prefix + adjstra- / super- / ultra- / arci-straricco, superveloce
Repetitionadjective + adjectivepiano piano (very slowly)
AdjectiveM. Sing.F. Sing.M. Pl.F. Pl.
bellobellissimobellissimabellissimibellissime
grandegrandissimograndissimagrandissimigrandissime
lungolunghissimolunghissimalunghissimilunghissime
felicefelicissimofelicissimafelicissimifelicissime
Adjectives ending in -co or -go add an h before -issimo to keep the hard sound: ricco → ricchissimo, largo → larghissimo, lungo → lunghissimo.
Il Monte Bianco è la montagna più alta di tutte le Alpi e d'Europa occidentale.
Mont Blanc is the tallest mountain in all the Alps and in Western Europe.
Questa è stata la giornata più calda dell'anno, con temperature che hanno superato i quaranta gradi.
This was the hottest day of the year, with temperatures exceeding forty degrees.
La pizza che abbiamo mangiato ieri sera a Napoli era buonissima e ne voglio un'altra.
The pizza we ate last night in Naples was extremely good and I want another one.
Il film è stato molto interessante, anche se era un po' lungo per i miei gusti.
The film was very interesting, even though it was a bit long for my taste.
La nonna cammina piano piano lungo il sentiero del giardino ogni mattina dopo colazione.
Grandma walks very slowly along the garden path every morning after breakfast.

My older brother works as an engineer in Turin and has lived there for five years.

There is not the slightest doubt that this is an excellent choice for the future of our company.
La situazione economica è pessima e dobbiamo fare il massimo sforzo per migliorarla rapidamente.
The economic situation is terrible and we must make the greatest effort to improve it quickly.
Aggettivi Possessivi — Possessive Adjectives
Italian possessive adjectives agree in gender and number with the thing possessed, not the possessor. They are normally preceded by the definite article, with important exceptions for family members.

Loro is invariable — it never changes form regardless of gender or number. It always requires the article.
Family Members — No Article (Singular Only)
With singular, unmodified family member nouns, the article is dropped (except with loro). The article returns in the plural or when the noun has a modifier (adjective, suffix, etc.).

Their mother is a very good history teacher and is respected by all the students.

Aggettivi Dimostrativi — Demonstrative Adjectives
Demonstrative adjectives point to specific nouns. Questo (this/these) indicates something near the speaker, while quello (that/those) indicates something far from the speaker. They replace the article — you never use both together.
Questo (This / These) — Regular Forms
Questo has four regular forms plus an optional elision before vowels. It follows standard adjective agreement patterns.
Before ConsonantBefore Vowel
M. Sing.questo libroquest'uomo / questo uomo
F. Sing.questa casaquest'amica / questa amica
M. Pl.questi libri / questi uomini

The elided forms (quest'uomo, quest'amica) are common in speech and writing but the full forms (questo uomo, questa amica) are always acceptable.

Italian adjectives are central to expressive, natural-sounding speech. Mastering agreement, position, and the special forms of common adjectives will dramatically improve both your comprehension and fluency. Pay special attention to the di/che distinction in comparatives, the article rules with possessives and family members, and the parallel patterns shared by the definite articles, bello, and quello.
