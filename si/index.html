Costruzioni con Si

Italian Si Constructions — reflexive, reciprocal, impersonal, and passive uses
One of the trickiest topics in Italian: the same little word si does four completely different jobs
Overview: The Many Faces of Si
Si has four or more distinct uses in Italian — mastering them is essential for understanding everyday speech and writing.
The Four Main Types of Si
The particle si appears constantly in Italian, but its meaning changes dramatically depending on context. Learning to distinguish these four uses is one of the most important steps toward fluency.
Type Name Meaning Example Translation
Reflexive Si riflessivo himself / herself / itself / themselves Marco si lava. Marco washes himself.
Reciprocal Si reciproco each other / one another Marco e Lucia si amano. Marco and Lucia love each other.
Impersonal Si impersonale one / you (generic) / people / they Si mangia bene qui. One eats well here. / You eat well here.
Passive Si passivante passive construction (thing is the subject) Si vendono case. Houses are sold.
Quick Comparison Examples
Notice how the same word si completely changes the meaning of each sentence below.
REFLEXIVE La ragazza si veste rapidamente ogni mattina prima di uscire di casa per andare al lavoro.
The girl dresses herself quickly every morning before leaving the house to go to work.
RECIPROCAL I due fratelli si scrivono lunghe lettere ogni settimana anche se vivono in due città diverse.
The two brothers write to each other long letters every week even though they live in two different cities.
IMPERSONAL In Italia si cena molto più tardi rispetto agli altri paesi europei, di solito verso le otto.
In Italy one dines much later compared to other European countries, usually around eight.
PASSIVE In questa libreria si trovano molti libri rari che non sono disponibili in nessun altro negozio.
In this bookshop many rare books are found that are not available in any other store.
Why is this so tricky? In English, each of these ideas uses a completely different structure (himself, each other, one/you, passive voice). In Italian, they all use the same tiny word: si. Context is everything.
Si Impersonale (Impersonal Si)
Equivalent to English "one," "you" (generic), "people," or "they" (impersonal) — extremely common in everyday Italian.
How It Works
Formula: si + 3rd person singular verb
Meaning: A generic, unspecified "someone" — no particular person is performing the action
English equivalents: "one does," "you do" (generic), "people do," "they do" (impersonal)
This construction is far more common in Italian than "one" is in English. Italians use it constantly where English speakers would use passive voice or generic "you."
Tense Patterns for Si Impersonale
The verb is always in the 3rd person singular, regardless of tense. In compound tenses, the auxiliary is always essere and the participle is masculine singular.
Tense Pattern Example Translation
Presente si + verb (3rd sing.) Si mangia bene. One eats well.
Imperfetto si + verb (3rd sing.) Si mangiava bene. One used to eat well.
Passato prossimo si + è + past participle (m. sing.) Si è mangiato bene. One ate well.
Futuro semplice si + verb (3rd sing.) Si mangerà bene. One will eat well.
Condizionale si + verb (3rd sing.) Si mangerebbe bene. One would eat well.
Congiuntivo pres. che si + verb (3rd sing.) Credo che si mangi bene. I think one eats well.
With Adjectives: The Plural Agreement Rule
When si impersonale is followed by essere + adjective, the adjective takes the plural form even though the verb is singular. This is because the impersonal "si" refers to people in general (a conceptually plural group).
Pattern: si + è + adjective (plural!)
ADJ RULE Quando si è stanchi dopo una lunga giornata di lavoro, non si ha voglia di cucinare la cena.
When one is tired after a long workday, one doesn't feel like cooking dinner. (stanchi = plural, not stanco!)
ADJ RULE Quando si è giovani non si pensa mai alle conseguenze delle proprie azioni quotidiane.
When one is young, one never thinks about the consequences of one's daily actions. (giovani = plural!)
Examples in Context
In questo ristorante si mangia molto bene e i prezzi sono davvero ragionevoli per la qualità offerta.
In this restaurant one eats very well and the prices are really reasonable for the quality offered.
Non si sa mai cosa può succedere nella vita, per questo bisogna essere sempre pronti a tutto.
One never knows what can happen in life, that's why one must always be ready for anything.
Quando si vive all'estero per molto tempo, si impara ad apprezzare cose che prima davamo per scontate.
When one lives abroad for a long time, one learns to appreciate things we used to take for granted.
Si dice che il clima stia cambiando rapidamente e che le temperature continueranno a salire nei prossimi anni.
They say / It is said that the climate is changing rapidly and that temperatures will continue to rise in the coming years.
In Italia si beve il caffè al bancone del bar, perché sedersi al tavolo costa di più.
In Italy one drinks coffee at the bar counter, because sitting at a table costs more.
D'estate si è mangiato quasi sempre all'aperto, nel giardino dietro la casa dei miei genitori.
In summer we (people) ate almost always outdoors, in the garden behind my parents' house.
Key point: Si impersonale always uses the 3rd person singular verb. Even though you're talking about people in general, the verb stays singular. The adjective, however, goes plural. This mismatch is one of the trickiest parts of Italian grammar.
Si Passivante (Passive Si)
Si + verb + subject (the subject is a thing, not a person) — the verb agrees with the subject. Very common in signs, ads, and announcements.
How It Works
Formula: si + verb (agrees with subject) + subject
Meaning: The subject (a thing) undergoes the action — equivalent to English passive voice
Key difference from si impersonale: In si passivante, the verb agrees in number with its subject (singular or plural). In si impersonale, the verb is always singular.
Think of it as: "Houses sell themselves" = "Houses are sold"
Singular vs Plural Subject Agreement
The verb must match the subject. When the subject is plural, the verb is plural too. This is the hallmark that distinguishes si passivante from si impersonale.
Subject Number Pattern Example Translation
Singular subject si + verb (3rd sing.) + noun (sing.) Si parla italiano. Italian is spoken.
Plural subject si + verb (3rd pl.) + noun (pl.) Si parlano due lingue. Two languages are spoken.
Singular subject si + verb (3rd sing.) + noun (sing.) Si vende questa casa. This house is sold / for sale.
Plural subject si + verb (3rd pl.) + noun (pl.) Si vendono appartamenti. Apartments are sold / for sale.
Singular subject si + verb (3rd sing.) + noun (sing.) Si affitta camera. Room for rent.
Plural subject si + verb (3rd pl.) + noun (pl.) Si affittano camere. Rooms for rent.
Compound Tenses with Si Passivante
In compound tenses, si passivante always uses essere as the auxiliary and the past participle agrees in gender and number with the subject.
Subject Passato Prossimo Translation
Masc. sing. Si è venduto il quadro. The painting was sold.
Fem. sing. Si è venduta la casa. The house was sold.
Masc. pl. Si sono venduti i quadri. The paintings were sold.
Fem. pl. Si sono vendute le case. The houses were sold.
Examples in Context
In questa pasticceria si preparano dolci artigianali ogni giorno utilizzando solo ingredienti freschi e naturali.
In this pastry shop artisanal sweets are prepared every day using only fresh and natural ingredients.
Nella nostra scuola si studiano tre lingue straniere: inglese, francese e tedesco, fin dal primo anno.
In our school three foreign languages are studied: English, French, and German, from the first year.
Si cercano camerieri con esperienza per il nuovo ristorante che aprirà il mese prossimo in centro città.
Experienced waiters are sought for the new restaurant that will open next month in the city center.
In quel negozio si vendono prodotti biologici che provengono direttamente dalle fattorie della regione toscana.
In that shop organic products are sold that come directly from farms in the Tuscan region.
Queste canzoni si ascoltano molto spesso alla radio durante la stagione estiva in tutta Italia.
These songs are listened to very often on the radio during the summer season throughout Italy.
Le case si sono vendute molto rapidamente perché i prezzi erano particolarmente bassi rispetto al mercato attuale.
The houses were sold very quickly because the prices were particularly low compared to the current market.
Spotting si passivante in the wild: Look for signs and ads! Si affitta (For rent), Si vende (For sale), Si cercano camerieri (Waiters wanted), Si accettano carte di credito (Credit cards accepted). These are all si passivante — the thing being rented, sold, sought, or accepted is the grammatical subject.
Si Reflexive vs Si Impersonale — How to Tell Them Apart
The same word "si" can be reflexive (himself/herself) or impersonal (one/people). Context is the key to telling them apart.
Side-by-Side Comparison
The critical question: Is there a specific person performing the action on themselves? If yes, it is reflexive. If no one specific is mentioned, it is impersonal.
Sentence Type Why? Translation
Maria si lava. Reflexive Maria is washing herself Maria washes herself.
Si lava la macchina qui. Impersonal No specific person — generic "one" One washes the car here.
Il bambino si sveglia. Reflexive The child wakes himself up The child wakes up.
Si sveglia presto in campagna. Impersonal No specific person — generic One wakes up early in the countryside.
Lei si diverte alla festa. Reflexive She is enjoying herself She is having fun at the party.
Si diverte molto in vacanza. Impersonal No specific person — people in general One has a lot of fun on vacation.
The "Ci Si" Rule: Impersonal + Reflexive Verbs
When si impersonale is used with a reflexive verb (which already has its own "si"), you cannot say "si si" — that sounds terrible in Italian. Instead, the first "si" becomes ci.
Pattern: ci si + verb (3rd person singular)
Logic: si (impersonal) + si (reflexive) = ci si
CI SI Ci si alza molto presto la mattina quando si lavora in una panetteria del centro storico.
One gets up very early in the morning when one works in a bakery in the old town center.
CI SI Ci si abitua rapidamente al nuovo stile di vita quando si hanno amici che vivono già nella stessa città.
One gets used to the new lifestyle quickly when one has friends who already live in the same city.
CI SI D'estate ci si diverte moltissimo al mare con gli amici, nuotando e giocando a pallavolo sulla spiaggia.
In summer one has a lot of fun at the seaside with friends, swimming and playing volleyball on the beach.
Contrastive Examples
REFLEXIVE Mia sorella si prepara sempre con grande cura prima di uscire la sera con le sue amiche.
My sister always gets herself ready with great care before going out in the evening with her friends.
IMPERSONAL Ci si prepara a lungo per un colloquio di lavoro importante, perché la prima impressione conta moltissimo.
One prepares oneself a long time for an important job interview, because the first impression matters a great deal.
REFLEXIVE Il nonno si siede sempre sulla stessa poltrona vicino alla finestra del soggiorno per leggere il giornale.
Grandpa always sits down on the same armchair near the living room window to read the newspaper.
IMPERSONAL In autobus ci si siede dove si trova un posto libero, senza scegliere troppo il sedile.
On the bus one sits wherever one finds a free seat, without choosing the seat too much.
REFLEXIVE I ragazzi si lavano le mani prima di sedersi a tavola ogni giorno, come hanno insegnato i genitori.
The kids wash their hands before sitting down at the table every day, as their parents taught them.
Remember: If you see a specific subject (Maria, il bambino, lei, i ragazzi), the si is reflexive. If there is no specific subject and the meaning is "one / people in general," it is impersonal. With reflexive verbs used impersonally, always use ci si — never "si si."
Si with Compound Tenses
All si constructions use essere as auxiliary in compound tenses — but the past participle agreement rules differ for each type.
Agreement Rules by Type
This is one of the most confusing areas. Every type of si uses essere in compound tenses, but the past participle agrees differently depending on which type of "si" you're dealing with.
Type Auxiliary Participle Agreement Example Translation
Si impersonale essere masculine singular (always) Si è mangiato bene. One ate well.
Si passivante (m. sing.) essere agrees with subject Si è venduto il quadro. The painting was sold.
Si passivante (f. sing.) essere agrees with subject Si è venduta la casa. The house was sold.
Si passivante (m. pl.) essere agrees with subject Si sono venduti molti libri. Many books were sold.
Si passivante (f. pl.) essere agrees with subject Si sono vendute molte case. Many houses were sold.
Si reflexive (f. sing.) essere agrees with subject Maria si è lavata. Maria washed herself.
Si reflexive (m. pl.) essere agrees with subject I ragazzi si sono lavati. The boys washed themselves.
Ci si (impersonal reflexive) essere masculine plural Ci si è divertiti molto. One had a lot of fun.
Examples in Passato Prossimo
IMPERSONALE Ieri sera si è mangiato benissimo al ristorante nuovo che hanno aperto vicino alla stazione centrale della città.
Last night one ate very well at the new restaurant they opened near the city's central station. (mangiato = m. sing. always)
PASSIVANTE Le ultime bottiglie di vino pregiato si sono vendute in meno di un'ora alla fiera del vino regionale.
The last bottles of fine wine were sold in less than an hour at the regional wine fair. (vendute = f. pl. agrees with bottiglie)
REFLEXIVE La bambina si è vestita da sola questa mattina per la prima volta, e i genitori erano molto orgogliosi di lei.
The little girl dressed herself alone this morning for the first time, and the parents were very proud of her. (vestita = f. sing. agrees with bambina)
CI SI Quest'estate ci si è divertiti tantissimo al festival musicale che si tiene ogni anno nel parco della città.
This summer one had a great time at the music festival that is held every year in the city park. (divertiti = m. pl.)
PASSIVANTE Tutti i biglietti per il concerto di domani si sono esauriti nel giro di pochi minuti dall'apertura delle vendite online.
All tickets for tomorrow's concert sold out within a few minutes of the online sales opening. (esauriti = m. pl. agrees with biglietti)
The golden rule: With si impersonale the participle is always masculine singular (si è mangiato, si è parlato, si è dormito). With si passivante and si reflexive the participle agrees with the subject. With ci si (impersonal reflexive) the participle is masculine plural (ci si è divertiti, ci si è alzati).
Common Si Expressions
Fixed phrases and idiomatic expressions using si that you will hear and read constantly in everyday Italian.

Examples in Context
Si dice che il nuovo direttore sia molto severo, ma nessuno lo conosce ancora abbastanza bene per giudicare.
They say that the new director is very strict, but nobody knows him well enough yet to judge.
Si sa che imparare una lingua straniera richiede tempo, pazienza e molta pratica quotidiana per migliorare davvero.
It is known that learning a foreign language requires time, patience, and a lot of daily practice to truly improve.
Si vede che hai studiato molto per questo esame, perché le tue risposte erano precise e ben argomentate.
You can tell that you studied a lot for this exam, because your answers were precise and well-argued.

Sorry, how do you say "good morning" in Japanese? I'm asking because next month I'm going to Tokyo for work.
Porto sempre l'ombrello quando esco di casa, anche se il cielo è sereno — non si sa mai con il tempo!
I always bring the umbrella when I leave the house, even if the sky is clear — you never know with the weather!
Come si fa a preparare una buona carbonara senza usare la panna? Te lo spiego io: si fa così, con uova e pecorino.
How do you make a good carbonara without using cream? I'll explain: this is how you do it, with eggs and pecorino.
Tip: These expressions are among the first "si" constructions you should memorize. They appear in everyday conversation, newspapers, and on TV. Once you recognize them, you'll hear them everywhere.
Common Mistakes
The most frequent errors learners make with si constructions — and how to fix them.
1. Using "si si" Instead of "ci si"
When using si impersonale with a reflexive verb, the impersonal "si" must change to ci. You can never have two "si" particles next to each other.
Si si alza presto la mattina quando si deve andare a lavorare in fabbrica alle sei.
WRONG — "si si" is never correct in Italian!
Ci si alza presto la mattina quando si deve andare a lavorare in fabbrica alle sei.
CORRECT — One gets up early in the morning when one has to go work in the factory at six.
2. Wrong Agreement with Si Passivante
With si passivante, the verb must agree with the subject (the thing). Learners often keep the verb singular when the subject is plural.
In questo negozio si vende prodotti biologici provenienti dalle fattorie locali della regione.
WRONG — "prodotti" is plural, so the verb must be plural too!
In questo negozio si vendono prodotti biologici provenienti dalle fattorie locali della regione.
CORRECT — "si vendono" agrees with the plural subject "prodotti."
3. Confusing Si Impersonale and Si Passivante
Si impersonale (verb always singular, no "thing" subject) vs si passivante (verb agrees with the "thing" subject). The distinction matters for verb agreement and participle agreement in compound tenses.
IMPERSONALE In Italia si mangia bene — there is no "thing" subject; "bene" is an adverb, not a noun.
Verb is singular (mangia) because this is impersonal — no subject to agree with.
PASSIVANTE In Italia si mangiano molti tipi di pasta ogni giorno in tutte le regioni del paese.
Verb is plural (mangiano) because "molti tipi di pasta" is the plural subject being acted upon.
4. Using Avere Instead of Essere in Compound Tenses
All si constructions use essere in compound tenses — even verbs that normally take avere (like mangiare, parlare, dormire).
Ieri sera si ha mangiato benissimo al ristorante che ci avevano consigliato i nostri amici.
WRONG — mangiare normally takes avere, but with "si" you must use essere!
Ieri sera si è mangiato benissimo al ristorante che ci avevano consigliato i nostri amici.
CORRECT — si constructions always use essere, regardless of the verb.
5. Wrong Participle Agreement with Ci Si
With "ci si" (impersonal reflexive) in compound tenses, the past participle takes the masculine plural ending. Learners often use the masculine singular.
Quest'estate ci si è divertito molto durante le vacanze al mare con tutti gli amici.
WRONG — "divertito" is masculine singular, but ci si requires masculine plural!
Quest'estate ci si è divertiti molto durante le vacanze al mare con tutti gli amici.
CORRECT — "divertiti" is masculine plural, as required by the ci si construction.
Summary of pitfalls: (1) Never write "si si" — use ci si. (2) Si passivante verb agrees with its subject. (3) Si impersonale verb is always singular. (4) Always use essere (never avere) in compound tenses with si. (5) Ci si participle is masculine plural.
Costruzioni con Si — One of the most important and nuanced topics in Italian grammar. Master these patterns and you will sound significantly more natural in both spoken and written Italian.
