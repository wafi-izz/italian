Italian Nouns — Gender, Plurals, Irregularities & Suffixes
Il Genere — Gender Rules
Every Italian noun has a grammatical gender: masculine (maschile) or feminine (femminile). Unlike English, gender affects articles, adjectives, and pronouns that accompany the noun. While there are reliable patterns, many exceptions exist — and some must simply be memorized.
Regular Ending Patterns
The most fundamental rule: nouns ending in -o are typically masculine, and nouns ending in -a are typically feminine. Nouns ending in -e can be either gender and must be learned individually.
EndingGenderExamples
-oMasculineil libro (book), il gatto (cat), il ragazzo (boy), il tavolo (table)
-aFemininela casa (house), la ragazza (girl), la porta (door), la finestra (window)
-eEitheril cane (dog) ♂ / la notte (night) ♀ / il ponte (bridge) ♂ / la chiave (key) ♀
Nouns Ending in -e
Nouns ending in -e require extra attention because gender is unpredictable from the ending alone. However, certain sub-endings provide reliable clues.
Sub-endingGenderExamples

-oreMasculineil colore (color), il dottore (doctor), il fiore (flower), l'amore (love), il sapore (flavor)
-menteFemininela mente (mind)
-ameMasculineil bestiame (livestock), il legname (timber)
-aleMasculineil giornale (newspaper), l'ospedale (hospital), il canale (canal)
-ileMasculineil fucile (rifle), il cortile (courtyard)

Examples
Gender by ending: Il ragazzo ha comprato un libro nuovo alla libreria del centro storico.
The boy bought a new book at the bookshop in the old town center.
-ione = feminine: La situazione economica in Italia è cambiata molto negli ultimi dieci anni.
The economic situation in Italy has changed a lot in the last ten years.
-ma = masculine: Il problema principale è che non abbiamo abbastanza tempo per finire il progetto.
The main problem is that we don't have enough time to finish the project.
-ista = both: La giornalista ha intervistato il famoso artista davanti al museo di arte moderna.
The journalist (f.) interviewed the famous artist (m.) in front of the modern art museum.
Foreign word: Ieri sera abbiamo guardato un film americano molto interessante al cinema.
Last night we watched a very interesting American film at the cinema.
-ore = masculine: Il colore del cielo al tramonto era un arancione intenso e bellissimo.
The color of the sky at sunset was an intense and very beautiful orange.
Tip: When learning a new noun, always memorize it together with its article (il, la, lo, l') — this is the most reliable way to remember the gender.
Il Plurale — Plural Formation
Italian nouns form their plurals by changing the final vowel, not by adding -s as in English. The change depends on the gender and ending of the singular noun. Mastering these patterns is essential because articles and adjectives must agree in both gender and number.
Regular Plural Patterns
The three core rules cover the vast majority of Italian nouns. Learn these first before tackling the exceptions.

Special Cases: -ca/-ga and -co/-go
Nouns ending in -ca and -ga add an h before the plural ending to preserve the hard /k/ and /g/ sounds. Nouns ending in -co and -go are trickier: some keep the hard sound (-chi/-ghi) and others shift to a soft sound (-ci/-gi). As a rough guide, words with stress on the second-to-last syllable tend to use -chi/-ghi, while words stressed on the third-to-last syllable tend to use -ci/-gi.

Words Ending in -io
When the i in -io is unstressed, the plural is simply -i (one i). When the i is stressed (accented: -ìo), the plural is -ii (two i's) or sometimes written with an accent (-ì).

Examples
-o → -i: Ho comprato tre libri nuovi per il corso di letteratura italiana all'università.
I bought three new books for the Italian literature course at the university.
-a → -e: Le case nel centro storico di Firenze sono molto antiche e hanno un fascino unico.
The houses in the old town center of Florence are very old and have a unique charm.
-ca → -che: Le mie amiche tedesche vengono a trovarmi in Italia durante le vacanze di Pasqua.
My German friends (f.) are coming to visit me in Italy during the Easter holidays.
-co → -ci: I migliori medici della città lavorano nel nuovo ospedale vicino alla stazione centrale.
The best doctors in the city work in the new hospital near the central station.
-ìo → -ii: I suoi zii vivono in una piccola casa di campagna vicino al lago di Garda.
His uncles live in a small country house near Lake Garda.
Tip: The -co/-go split is one of the most frustrating aspects for learners. When in doubt, check a dictionary — there is no single rule that covers all cases.
Plurali Irregolari — Irregular Plurals
Some Italian nouns form their plurals in completely unexpected ways. The most striking group involves masculine singular nouns that become feminine in the plural, often with the ending -a. These are remnants of the Latin neuter gender and must be memorized individually.
Gender-Changing Plurals (Masculine → Feminine)
These nouns are masculine singular (ending in -o) but become feminine plural (ending in -a), taking the feminine article le. They often refer to body parts or paired things.

Note: Some of these nouns also have a regular masculine plural with a different shade of meaning. For example: i muri (walls of a house) vs. le mura (walls of a city); i gridi (animal cries) vs. le grida (human screams).

These nouns do not follow any predictable pattern. Their plural forms must simply be memorized.

Gender change: Mi fanno male le braccia dopo aver portato tutte quelle borse pesanti dal supermercato.
My arms hurt after carrying all those heavy bags from the supermarket.
Gender change: Le uova fresche che compriamo al mercato del contadino sono molto più buone di quelle del supermercato.
The fresh eggs we buy at the farmer's market are much better than those from the supermarket.
Irregular: Gli uomini seduti al bar discutevano animatamente della partita di calcio di ieri sera.
The men sitting at the bar were animatedly discussing last night's football match.
Gender change: Ho bisogno di due paia di scarpe nuove perché le mie vecchie sono completamente consumate.
I need two pairs of new shoes because my old ones are completely worn out.
Irregular: Si è lavata le mani con acqua calda e sapone prima di sedersi a tavola con la famiglia.
She washed her hands with hot water and soap before sitting down at the table with the family.
Warning: Gender-changing plurals are one of the hardest aspects of Italian nouns. Even advanced learners occasionally slip up. Pay special attention to body part vocabulary!
Nomi Invariabili — Invariable Nouns
Some Italian nouns have the same form in both singular and plural. The number is determined only by the article and any accompanying adjectives. These fall into several predictable categories.
Words Ending in an Accented Vowel
Nouns whose final vowel carries a written accent never change in the plural. The article is the only indicator of number.

Examples
Accented vowel: In Italia ci sono molte università prestigiose che attirano studenti da tutto il mondo.
In Italy there are many prestigious universities that attract students from all over the world.
Abbreviation: Ho scattato tantissime foto durante il viaggio in Costiera Amalfitana la settimana scorsa.
I took so many photos during the trip to the Amalfi Coast last week.
Foreign word: I bar italiani sono molto diversi dai bar americani perché servono soprattutto caffè e cornetti.
Italian bars are very different from American bars because they mainly serve coffee and croissants.
Abbreviation: Le auto parcheggiate lungo la strada impedivano il passaggio dei pedoni sul marciapiede.
The cars parked along the road were blocking the pedestrians' passage on the sidewalk.
One-syllable: Nella storia d'Italia ci sono stati molti re che hanno governato con grande saggezza e coraggio.
In the history of Italy there have been many kings who ruled with great wisdom and courage.
Tip: When you see a noun that doesn't change in the plural, rely on the article (il → i, la → le, lo → gli, l' → gli/le) and adjective agreement to determine whether the meaning is singular or plural.
Trappole di Genere — Common Gender Traps
These are the nouns that consistently trip up learners because their gender contradicts the expected pattern. Some masculine words look feminine, some feminine words look masculine, and some words change meaning entirely depending on which gender you assign them.

Examples
Masc. in -a: Il clima mediterraneo dell'Italia meridionale attira milioni di turisti stranieri ogni estate.
The Mediterranean climate of southern Italy attracts millions of foreign tourists every summer.
Fem. in -o: Mi sono bruciata la mano sinistra mentre stavo cucinando la cena per tutta la famiglia.
I burned my left hand while I was cooking dinner for the whole family.
il fine vs la fine: Il fine del progetto è migliorare la qualità della vita nei quartieri più poveri della città.
The purpose of the project is to improve the quality of life in the poorest neighborhoods of the city.
il fine vs la fine: La fine del film mi ha commosso così tanto che non sono riuscito a trattenere le lacrime.
The end of the film moved me so much that I couldn't hold back my tears.
il capitale vs la capitale: Roma è la capitale d'Italia e una delle città più visitate al mondo per la sua storia millenaria.
Rome is the capital of Italy and one of the most visited cities in the world for its thousand-year history.
il fronte vs la fronte: Il soldato aveva una cicatrice sulla fronte che gli ricordava gli orrori della guerra ogni giorno.
The soldier had a scar on his forehead that reminded him of the horrors of war every day.
Warning: The -ma words (problema, sistema, tema, etc.) are by far the most common gender trap. Train yourself to say "il problema" until it becomes automatic — saying "la problema" is a telltale beginner mistake.
Suffissi — Suffixes
Italian has a rich system of suffixes (suffissi alterativi) that modify the meaning of a noun without changing its core identity. They can express smallness, endearment, largeness, ugliness, or contempt. Using them correctly adds warmth and color to your Italian, but using them incorrectly can sound awkward or even offensive.
Diminutivi — Diminutives (Smallness / Endearment)
Diminutive suffixes make a noun smaller or express affection and tenderness. The choice of suffix depends on the word's ending and on regional preference or personal style. The modified noun keeps the gender of the original or takes the gender of the suffix.

Accrescitivi — Augmentatives (Largeness / Emphasis)
Augmentative suffixes make a noun bigger or emphasize its size, intensity, or importance. The most common suffix is -one. Note that when added to a feminine noun, the result is often masculine (la donna → il donnone).
SuffixBase WordModified FormMeaning
-one / -onail libro (book)il libronebig book, tome
-one / -onala donna (woman)il donnonebig/large woman
-one / -onail naso (nose)il nasonebig nose
-one / -onala porta (door)il portonemain entrance, large door
-one / -onala palla (ball)il pallonefootball, large ball
-one / -onala minestra (soup)il minestronethick vegetable soup
Note: Some augmentatives have become standard words with fixed meanings: il portone (main entrance door), il minestrone (thick soup), il pallone (football) — these are no longer felt as "big" versions of the base word.
Peggiorativi — Pejoratives (Ugliness / Contempt)
Pejorative suffixes add a negative connotation — expressing that something is bad, ugly, unpleasant, or of poor quality. Use them with care, as they can be rude when applied to people.

Examples
Diminutive -ino: Il gattino che abbiamo adottato dal rifugio si è subito nascosto sotto il divano del soggiorno.
The kitten we adopted from the shelter immediately hid under the living room couch.
Diminutive -etta: Abbiamo affittato una casetta in montagna dove passeremo le vacanze di Natale con tutta la famiglia.
We rented a little house in the mountains where we'll spend the Christmas holidays with the whole family.
Augmentative -one: Ha un nasone enorme che lo rende immediatamente riconoscibile in mezzo alla folla.
He has an enormous big nose that makes him immediately recognizable in a crowd.
Pejorative -accia: I bambini non dovrebbero mai dire le parolacce, soprattutto davanti agli adulti e agli insegnanti.
Children should never say swear words, especially in front of adults and teachers.
Pejorative -accio: Che tempaccio orribile oggi — piove a dirotto e tira un vento fortissimo da stamattina.
What awful weather today — it's pouring rain and there's been a very strong wind since this morning.
Tip: Not all nouns accept all suffixes — the combinations are largely idiomatic. For instance, you cannot say *"finestrone" for a big window (you'd say "una finestra grande"). When in doubt, use an adjective (grande, piccolo, brutto) instead of a suffix.
