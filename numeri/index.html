Numeri e Tempo

Italian Numbers, Dates, Time & Counting Expressions
Numeri Cardinali — Cardinal Numbers
Cardinal numbers are the basic counting numbers. Italian numbers are largely regular, but the first twenty and multiples of ten have unique forms that must be memorized.
0–20: The Foundation
These are the building blocks. Numbers 0–10 are completely unique; 11–16 have the -dici suffix; 17–19 use dici- as a prefix. Venti (20) stands alone.

Tens: 20–100
The tens are regular from 20 onward. When combining with uno or otto, the final vowel of the ten is dropped: ventuno (21), ventotto (28), trentuno (31), quarantotto (48). With tre, add an accent: ventitré, trentatré.

Pattern for combining: venti + due = ventidue (22), trenta + cinque = trentacinque (35), quaranta + sei = quarantasei (46). But: venti + uno = ventuno (drop the -i), venti + otto = ventotto.
100–1,000,000+
Cento (100) doesn't change in the plural: duecento, trecento. Mille (1,000) becomes mila in the plural: duemila, tremila. Milione and miliardo are nouns and require di before the thing being counted.

Numeri Ordinali — Ordinal Numbers
Ordinal numbers indicate position or order. Unlike cardinals, they agree in gender and number with the noun they modify, like adjectives: primo/prima/primi/prime.
1st–10th: Unique Forms
The first ten ordinals have unique forms that must be memorized. From 11th onward, drop the final vowel of the cardinal and add -esimo (with gender/number agreement).

Centuries & Popes: For centuries, use ordinals: il Quattrocento (the 1400s/15th century), il Cinquecento (the 1500s/16th century). For popes and kings, use Roman numerals: Giovanni Paolo II (Secondo), Papa Francesco (no numeral for the first).
L'Ora — Telling Time
Italian uses the verb "essere" (to be) for telling time. Hours are feminine because "ora" (hour) is implied. Italy commonly uses the 24-hour clock in formal contexts.
Basic Structure
Use è for 1:00 (l'una) and midnight/noon. Use sono for all other hours. The article le precedes the number (except l'una, mezzogiorno, mezzanotte).

Minutes and Precision
Add minutes with e (and): le due e dieci (2:10). Subtract minutes from the next hour with meno (minus): le tre meno venti (2:40, literally "three minus twenty").

Asking and Answering
The standard question is Che ora è? or Che ore sono? (What time is it?). To say "at" a specific time, use a/alle: alle due (at 2:00), all'una (at 1:00), a mezzogiorno (at noon).

12h vs 24h: In everyday speech, Italians often use the 12-hour clock with context (di mattina, di pomeriggio, di sera, di notte). In writing, schedules, and official contexts, the 24-hour clock is standard: le 14:30, le 20:00.
Le Date — Dates, Days & Months
Italian dates use cardinal numbers (not ordinals, except for the first of the month). The format is: day/month/year, with the definite article before the number.
Days of the Week
Days are not capitalized in Italian. Monday through Friday are masculine; Saturday and Sunday are also masculine. The week starts on Monday (lunedì) in Italy.

Habitual days: Use the definite article to express "every": il lunedì = every Monday, la domenica = every Sunday. Without the article, it means "this/next" specific day.
Months of the Year
Months are also not capitalized in Italian. They are all masculine. Use in or a for "in (month)": in gennaio or a gennaio.

Quantità e Misure — Quantities & Measurements
Fractions, decimals, percentages, and mathematical operations follow specific patterns in Italian. Note: Italy uses a comma for decimals and a period for thousands (opposite of English).
Fractions
Fractions use cardinal number + ordinal number: un terzo (1/3), due quinti (2/5). Half is special: mezzo/mezza (adjective) or la metà (noun).

Espressioni Numeriche — Useful Number Expressions
Common phrases and expressions involving numbers that you'll encounter in daily Italian conversation, from age to phone numbers to addresses.
Age, Phone Numbers & Addresses
Age uses avere + number + anni (to have years). Phone numbers are read digit by digit or in pairs. Addresses put the number after the street name.
